Start with one useful meal.

No big launch needed. A household, club, street, school group or caring circle can start with one private board, one small cook-up and a clear safety check.

Private group noticeboards are the daily tool.

Public screens are useful later. The first living version is smaller and warmer: a private board for a family cluster, neighbours, a club kitchen, a youth group, elders support, a disaster-ready street or a produce-sharing circle.

Each group can keep its own food list, meal ideas, roster and check-in notes without turning people's lives into public content.

Private by default

  • Names, addresses, health details and donor lists stay inside the trusted group.
  • Public notices only show the general call-out, time, place, expiry and contact path.
  • Food safety and cultural permission are checked before anything becomes a public story.

Kitchen Mate is just good prompting.

You do not need a special app to start. Copy a prompt into ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini or whatever AI people already use, then have a human check the result.

Meal from surplus

I have [ingredients]. We are cooking for [number]. Make a simple safe meal plan with allergy questions, prep steps, storage notes and no fancy ingredients.

Produce board post

Write a friendly private group noticeboard post asking who can use or add [produce]. Include pickup timing, expiry, safety notes and a respectful tone.

Cook-up roster

Turn this meal idea into a roster for shopping gaps, prep, cooking, serving, cleanup, leftovers and one person checking allergies before food is shared.

Local resilience is made before the emergency.

The disaster kiosk research makes the point plainly: emergency tools work best when people already use them in normal life. Shared Table does that with food. It gives people a reason to practise coordination, care, notices, rosters and local resource sharing before the hard day arrives.

That is the quiet power here. Not hype. Neighbours knowing who has a freezer, who can drive, who can cook, who needs help, and how to ask without shame.
